Deep Literature Review: Punctuated Infectiousness

Compiled 2026-03-13. Based on training knowledge through May 2025.

1. Individual vs. Population Infectiousness Profiles

Fraser, C. (2007). "Estimating Individual and Household Reproduction Numbers in an Emerging Epidemic."
PLoS ONE, 2(8), e758. doi:10.1371/journal.pone.0000758

Relevance: Defines the individual infectiousness kernel $\beta(s, t)$, essentially the $a_i(\tau)$ concept. Focuses on estimating $R_i$ (the individual reproduction number), not on the shape of the individual kernel or how variation in kernel shape affects dynamics. Does not parameterize a family of individual kernels that interpolate between smooth and spiked while preserving the population average.

Relation to project: Closest prior work to the individual-level kernel concept. Your $a_i(\tau)$ and $E[a_i(\tau)] = A(\tau)$ formalize ideas implicit here. But Fraser never isolates temporal concentration as a parameter of interest.

Svensson, A. (2007). "A note on generation times in epidemic models."
Mathematical Biosciences, 208(1), 300-311. doi:10.1016/j.mbs.2006.10.010

Relevance: Carefully distinguishes generation time from latency/infectious period. Notes that the generation time distribution emerges from the interplay of individual-level disease dynamics. Does not explicitly parameterize individual-level vs. population-level generation intervals as distinct objects.

Champredon, D. & Dushoff, J. (2015). "Intrinsic and realized generation intervals in infectious-disease transmission."
Proc. R. Soc. B, 282(1821), 20152026. doi:10.1098/rspb.2015.2026

Relevance: Distinguishes intrinsic GI (in fully susceptible population) from realized (forward/backward) GI. Discusses $\beta(\tau)$ as the individual infectiousness profile. Focus is on how epidemic dynamics filter the intrinsic distribution, not on how the shape of individual profiles generates the population kernel. Does not consider multiple $a_i(\tau)$ averaging to the same $A(\tau)$.

Scalia Tomba, G., Svensson, A., Asikainen, T. & Giesecke, J. (2010). "Some model based considerations on observing generation times for communicable diseases."
Mathematical Biosciences, 223(1), 24-31. doi:10.1016/j.mbs.2009.10.004

Relevance: Discusses how observed generation times depend on individual infectiousness model. Notes generation time = convolution of latent period and relative infectivity. Comes close to noting that different individual-level models can produce the same population GI, but does not parameterize this non-identifiability.

Svensson, A. (2015). "The influence of assumptions on generation time distributions in epidemic models."
Mathematical Biosciences, 270, 81-89. doi:10.1016/j.mbs.2015.10.006

Relevance: Directly relevant. Shows that different assumptions about the individual infectious period (constant vs. exponential vs. other) lead to different generation time distributions. Closest prior work to the observation that multiple $a_i(\tau)$ can yield the same $A(\tau)$, though framed as an inference problem rather than examining dynamical consequences.

Breda, D., Diekmann, O., de Graaf, W.F., Pugliese, A. & Vermiglio, R. (2012). "On the formulation of epidemic models (an appraisal of Kermack and McKendrick)."
J. Biological Dynamics, 6(sup2), 103-117. doi:10.1080/17513758.2012.716454

Relevance: Mathematical foundation connecting renewal equation to ODE compartmental models via $A(\tau)$. Establishes SEIR = specific $A(\tau)$. Does not discuss individual-level variation in the kernel.

Novelty assessment for Angle 1: The concept that different individual profiles can yield the same population $A(\tau)$ is implicit in several papers, but no one has (a) explicitly parameterized a one-parameter family interpolating between smooth and spike while preserving $A(\tau)$, (b) used the Gamma convolution property to achieve this, or (c) studied the dynamical consequences. This is genuinely novel.

2. Linear Chain Trick, Erlang Models, and Narrow Infectious Periods

Lloyd, A.L. (2001a). "Realistic Distributions of Infectious Periods in Epidemic Models: Changing Patterns of Persistence and Dynamics."
Theoretical Population Biology, 60(1), 59-71. doi:10.1006/tpbi.2001.1525

Relevance: Highly relevant. Studies replacing exponential with Erlang infectious/latent periods. Narrower infectious period distributions destabilize deterministic dynamics. Uses linear chain trick. Lloyd's "many I stages" ~ large $\kappa$; "few I stages" ~ small $\kappa$.

Key differences: (1) Lloyd varies the population-level $A(\tau)$, you hold it fixed. (2) Deterministic focus, not stochastic. (3) No shared-shift + jitter decomposition.

Lloyd, A.L. (2001b). "Destabilization of epidemic models with the inclusion of realistic distributions of infectious periods."
Proc. R. Soc. B, 268(1470), 985-993. doi:10.1098/rspb.2001.1599

Relevance: Companion paper. Erlang infectious periods with same mean but lower variance destabilize endemic equilibrium. Mechanism operates through changing $A(\tau)$ itself.

Wearing, H.J., Rohani, P. & Keeling, M.J. (2005). "Appropriate Models for the Management of Infectious Diseases."
PLoS Medicine, 2(7), e174. doi:10.1371/journal.pmed.0020174

Relevance: Highly relevant. Distribution choice for infectious period substantially affects $R_0$ estimates, peak size, optimal intervention timing. Advocates non-exponential distributions. Varies population-level profile. Their finding that "distribution shape matters" motivates asking whether the individual-level decomposition matters too.

Yan, P. (2008). "Separate roles of the latent and infectious periods in shaping the relation between the basic reproduction number and the intrinsic growth rate."
J. Theoretical Biology, 251(2), 238-252. doi:10.1016/j.jtbi.2007.11.027

Relevance: Highly relevant. Decomposes generation interval into latent + infectious periods. Structurally similar to your shared-shift + jitter: latent ~ shared shift $s_i$, infectious ~ jitter. But Yan varies $A(\tau)$; you fix it. Yan is deterministic; you are stochastic. Yan shows: for given mean GI, more latent (vs. infectious) time increases growth rate.

Anderson, D. & Watson, R. (1980). "On the spread of a disease with gamma distributed latent and infectious periods."
Biometrika, 67(1), 191-198. doi:10.1093/biomet/67.1.191

Relevance: Early work on Gamma-distributed stages. Shows shape parameter affects dynamics.

Connection to $\kappa$: Your shared shift $s_i \sim \text{Gamma}(\alpha - \kappa, r)$ plays the role of latent period; jitter $\varepsilon_j \sim \text{Gamma}(\kappa, r)$ plays the role of infectious period. Small $\kappa$ = long latent + short infectious = punctuated. The linear chain trick literature has studied varying this split, but always at the population level (changing $A(\tau)$). Your key insight: varying the split while preserving $A(\tau)$ is possible and has distinct stochastic consequences.

3. Generation Interval Shape and Stochastic Epidemic Dynamics

Wallinga, J. & Lipsitch, M. (2007). "How generation intervals shape the relationship between growth rates and reproductive numbers."
Proc. R. Soc. B, 274(1609), 599-604. doi:10.1098/rspb.2006.3754

Relevance: Foundational. GI shape determines $r \to R_0$ mapping via Euler-Lotka. Your project shows $r$ and $R_0$ invariant to $\kappa$ while stochastic variability of epidemic timing depends on $\kappa$.

Park, S.W., Champredon, D., Weitz, J.S. & Dushoff, J. (2019). "A practical generation-interval-based approach to inferring the strength of epidemics from their speed."
Epidemics, 27, 12-18. doi:10.1016/j.epidem.2018.12.002

Relevance: Extends Wallinga-Lipsitch with practical methods. Establishes Gamma GI / Euler-Lotka machinery. Does not consider individual-level decompositions.

Park, S.W., Champredon, D. & Dushoff, J. (2020). "Inferring generation-interval distributions from contact-tracing data."
J. R. Soc. Interface, 17(167), 20190719. doi:10.1098/rsif.2019.0719

Relevance: How realized GIs differ from intrinsic. Does not parameterize individual-level variation in infectiousness shape.

Britton, T. & Scalia Tomba, G. (2019). "Estimation in emerging epidemics: biases and remedies."
J. R. Soc. Interface, 16(150), 20180670. doi:10.1098/rsif.2018.0670

Relevance: Estimation biases for GI and $R_0$. Relevant to identifiability section but does not address individual-level decomposition.

4. Superspreading in Time / Temporal Heterogeneity

This angle has the least prior literature. "Superspreading" has been used almost exclusively for variation in the number of offspring.

Lloyd-Smith, J.O., Schreiber, S.J., Kopp, P.E. & Getz, W.M. (2005). "Superspreading and the effect of individual variation on disease emergence."
Nature, 438, 355-359. doi:10.1038/nature04153

Relevance: Foundational for "number" dimension. Models $\nu_i$ as Gamma-distributed, yielding NegBin offspring with dispersion $k$. Does not consider temporal variation. Your overdispersion result (punctuated infectiousness + periodic contacts generates overdispersion without $\nu_i$ variation) is a new complementary mechanism.

Endo, A., Abbott, S., Kucharski, A.J. & Funk, S. (2020). "Estimating the overdispersion in COVID-19 transmission using outbreak sizes outside China."
Wellcome Open Research, 5, 67. doi:10.12688/wellcomeopenres.15842.3

Relevance: Estimates $k \approx 0.1$ for SARS-CoV-2. Attributes to individual transmissibility variation. Your framework offers an additional/alternative mechanism.

Goyal, A. et al. (2021). "Viral load and contact heterogeneity predict SARS-CoV-2 transmission and super-spreading events."
eLife, 10, e63537. doi:10.7554/eLife.63537

Relevance: Closest prior work to your contact-periodicity mechanism. Concentrated viral load + contact variation drives superspreading. But simulation-only, no analytical decomposition, no closed-form $k(\kappa)$.

Althouse, B.M. et al. (2020). "Superspreading events in the transmission dynamics of SARS-CoV-2."
PLoS Biology, 18(11), e3000897. doi:10.1371/journal.pbio.3000897

Relevance: Superspreading at gatherings. Transmission in "bursts." Related to your mechanism but framed as contact-setting heterogeneity, not formalized.

Novelty assessment for Angle 4: "Temporal superspreading" -- concentrated infectiousness interacting with variable contacts to generate overdispersed offspring -- appears genuinely novel as a formal mathematical result. Prior work focuses on $\nu_i$ heterogeneity. Your closed-form $k(\kappa, \text{contact parameters})$ is new.

5. The $W$/Time-Shift Framework

Morris, D., Maclean, J. & Black, A.J. (2024). "Computation of random time-shift distributions for stochastic population models."
J. Mathematical Biology, 89(3), 33. doi:10.1007/s00285-024-02132-6

Relevance: Directly relevant -- already cited. Efficient numerical methods for $W$. Does not study $W$'s dependence on individual-level kernel decomposition or parameterize by anything like $\kappa$.

Barbour, A.D. (1975). "The duration of the closed stochastic epidemic."
Biometrika, 62(2), 477-482. doi:10.1093/biomet/62.2.477

Relevance: Origin of the time-shift idea for stochastic epidemics.

Ball, F. (1983). "The threshold behaviour of epidemic models."
J. Applied Probability, 20(2), 227-241. doi:10.2307/3213797

Relevance: Establishes CMJ branching process approximation for early epidemic phase with general generation intervals.

Jagers, P. (1975). Branching Processes with Biological Applications.
Wiley. ISBN: 978-0471436525

Relevance: Foundational on CMJ processes. Defines $W = \lim_{t \to \infty} e^{-\alpha t} Z(t)$. Moments of $W$ depend on offspring point process.

Nerman, O. (1981). "On the convergence of supercritical general (C.M.J.) branching processes."
Z. Wahrscheinlichkeitstheorie verw. Gebiete, 57, 365-395.

Relevance: Convergence conditions for $W(t) \to W$. Technical foundation for $\text{Var}(W)$.

Jagers, P. & Nerman, O. (1984). "The growth and composition of branching populations."
Advances in Applied Probability, 16(2), 221-259.

Relevance: Formulas for $E[W^2]$ in terms of MGF of offspring point process. Your $\text{Var}(W)$ formula likely derives from these for the specific $\kappa$-parameterized family.

Assessment: Your closed-form $\text{Var}(W)$ as a function of $\kappa$ (preserving $A(\tau)$ and $R_0$) is a new result. The general CMJ theory provides tools, and Morris et al. (2024) provide computational methods, but the specific application to a $\kappa$-parameterized family preserving population-level statistics has not been done.

6. Interventions and Individual Infectiousness

Fraser, C., Riley, S., Anderson, R.M. & Ferguson, N.M. (2004). "Factors that make an infectious disease outbreak controllable."
PNAS, 101(16), 6146-6151. doi:10.1073/pnas.0307506101

Relevance: Highly relevant. Controllability depends on fraction of transmission before symptom onset ($\theta$). Your $F_\kappa(\delta_{\text{sym}})$ is their $\theta$ but parameterized by $\kappa$. Fraser et al. treat $\theta$ as fixed; you show it depends on $\kappa$.

Larremore, D.B. et al. (2021). "Test sensitivity is secondary to frequency and turnaround time for COVID-19 screening."
Science Advances, 7(1), eabd5393. doi:10.1126/sciadv.abd5393

Relevance: Screening frequency > sensitivity. Assumes smooth profile. Your work suggests the tradeoff is $\kappa$-dependent.

Middleton, C. & Larremore, D.B. (2024). "Modeling the transmission mitigation impact of testing for infectious diseases."
Science Advances, 10(24), eadk5108. doi:10.1126/sciadv.adk5108

Relevance: Testing effectiveness framework. Assumes smooth profile. Your work shows TE depends on $\kappa$.

Ferretti, L. et al. (2020). "Quantifying SARS-CoV-2 transmission suggests epidemic control with digital contact tracing."
Science, 368(6491), eabb6936. doi:10.1126/science.abb6936

Relevance: Decomposes transmission into timing components. Their $\beta(s)$ = your $a_i(\tau)$ averaged. Does not consider heterogeneity in profile shape.

Dushoff, J. & Park, S.W. (2021). "Speed and strength of an epidemic intervention."
Proc. R. Soc. B, 288(1947), 20201556. doi:10.1098/rspb.2020.1556

Relevance: Framework for comparing intervention effects on $R_{\text{eff}}$ ("strength") vs. growth rate ("speed"). Directly relevant to your "partially self-defeating intervention" result for smooth profiles under screening.

Cevik, M. et al. (2021). "SARS-CoV-2, SARS-CoV, and MERS-CoV viral load dynamics."
Lancet Microbe, 2(1), e13-e22.

Relevance: Viral shedding duration >> likely infectious period. Empirical support for punctuated infectiousness hypothesis.

Novelty for Angle 6: No paper systematically examines how individual-level concentration ($\kappa$) affects intervention effectiveness while holding $A(\tau)$ fixed. Your findings are novel.

7. Redundancy Assessment

No paper makes the core results redundant. Closest:
  • Yan (2008): Similar decomposition but changes $A(\tau)$; deterministic. Not redundant.
  • Lloyd (2001): Distribution shape matters but at population level. Not redundant.
  • Morris et al. (2024): Provides $W$ framework, does not study $W(\kappa)$. Not redundant.
  • Goyal et al. (2021): Similar mechanism, simulation-only. Not redundant.
  • Fraser (2007): Individual kernel concept but $\nu_i$ focus. Not redundant.
  • Svensson (2015): Notes model non-uniqueness, inference framing only. Not redundant.

8. Summary of Novelty

Genuinely novel aspects:
  1. Gamma convolutional framework: One-parameter family ($\kappa$) preserving $A(\tau)$ exactly via Gamma additivity.
  2. Stochastic dynamics: Closed-form $\text{Var}(W)$ as a function of $\kappa$. Mean-field invariance + stochastic $\kappa$-dependence.
  3. Temporal superspreading: Punctuated infectiousness + periodic contacts generates offspring overdispersion. Closed-form $k(\kappa)$.
  4. Intervention $\kappa$-dependence: $R_{\text{eff}}$ varies 1.6x across $\kappa$; all-or-nothing vs. partial detection; self-defeating intervention effect.
  5. $b_i \cdot c_i$ decomposition: Systematic analysis of how biological/contact split interacts with $\kappa$.
  6. Identifiability of $\kappa$: Entirely new question.

Why does κ=0.25 look so similar to the spike (κ=0)?

Var(W) is highly convex in κ

Your thinking is right that \(\kappa = 0.25\) makes the individual infectiousness profile's SD equal to half the population GI's SD (\(\sqrt{\kappa} = 0.5\)). But the effect on Var(\(W\)) is highly nonlinear — the extra variance beyond the smooth baseline drops like \(R_0^{-2\kappa}\), which is convex and front-loaded.

The key formula

The extra variance beyond the smooth limit decomposes as:

$$\Delta(\kappa) \;=\; \text{Var}(W)\big|_\kappa - \text{Var}(W)\big|_{\kappa=1} \;=\; \frac{R_0^2}{1 - m_2}\;\underbrace{\rho^{2\kappa\alpha_{\text{pop}}}}_{\text{prefactor}} \cdot \underbrace{\left(\rho_2^{(1-\kappa)\alpha_{\text{pop}}} - \rho^{2(1-\kappa)\alpha_{\text{pop}}}\right)}_{\text{bracket}}$$

Since \(\rho^{\alpha_{\text{pop}}} = 1/R_0\), the prefactor simplifies to:

$$\rho^{2\kappa\alpha_{\text{pop}}} = R_0^{-2\kappa}$$

This factor is exponential in \(\kappa\) and drops very steeply near \(\kappa = 0\):

\(\kappa\)\(R_0^{-2\kappa}\)Var(\(W\))% of spike extra var

0.100.6080.72088%
0.250.2890.60871%
0.500.0830.43844%
0.750.0240.28621%
10.0070.1500%

At \(\kappa = 0.25\), the individual profile has half the SD of the population GI — but 71% of the spike's extra variance in \(W\) is retained. You'd need roughly \(\kappa \approx 0.5\) for a halfway effect.

Intuition

The \(R_0^{-2\kappa}\) prefactor comes from the Laplace transform of the jitter component evaluated at \(2\alpha\). The jitter's shape is \(\kappa \alpha_{\text{pop}}\), and even a small shape parameter produces substantial "diversification" — reducing variance by spreading offspring times apart. But the marginal diversification benefit diminishes rapidly: the first 25% of jitter variance eliminates only 29% of the extra W-variance, while the last 25% eliminates 21%.

The effect is stronger at higher \(R_0\) because \(R_0^{-2\kappa}\) drops faster. For \(R_0 = 2\) (influenza), \(R_0^{-0.5} = 0.71\) — a modest drop. For \(R_0 = 12\) (measles), \(R_0^{-0.5} = 0.29\) — a steep drop but still leaving most of the extra variance. The net effect is that \(\kappa = 0.25\) stays close to the spike across all realistic \(R_0\).
