\section{Introduction}

Individual-level variation can have a profound impact on an epidemic's trajectory. Interpersonal variation in infectiousness determines how likely an epidemic is to become established and how explosive it may be \citep{lloyd-smith_superspreading_2005}. Likewise, individual-level stochastic effects early in an outbreak can durably shift the timing of an epidemic as a whole \citep{morris_computation_2024}. While the mean-field dynamics of an epidemic are dictated by two population-level quantities --- the basic reproduction number ($R_0$) and the intrinsic generation interval distribution ($g(\tau)$) \citep{wallinga_how_2007} --- stochastic departures from the mean depend on the individual-level heterogeneity from which these population-level quantities emerge. 
% arising for example from variable viral shedding or contact rates, 

Both $R_0$ and $g(\tau)$ are expectations of individual-level quantities: $R_0 = E_i[\nu_i]$, where $\nu_i$ is the individual reproduction number \citep{lloyd-smith_superspreading_2005}, and $g(\tau) = E_i[\xi_i(\tau)]$, where $\xi_i(\tau)$ is a probability density describing how person $i$'s secondary infections are distributed in time. The parameters $R_0$ and $g(\tau)$ can be combined into a single object, $A(\tau) = R_0\, g(\tau)$, which captures both the total expected transmission and its distribution over time \citep{breda_formulation_2012}. Analogously, the individual infectiousness profile or infectiousness kernel, $a_i(\tau) = \nu_i \xi_i(\tau)$, captures the expected number and timing of person $i$'s secondary infections \citep{champredon_intrinsic_2015, svensson_note_2007}. Substantial work has gone into understanding how variation in $\nu_i$ impacts epidemic dynamics; however, the impact of variation in $\xi_i(\tau)$ --- independent of $\nu_i$, $R_0$, and $g(\tau)$ --- is less well developed.  

% can be combined into a single object: the infectiousness profile $A(\tau) = R_0\,g(\tau)$, which captures both the total expected transmission and its distribution over time \citep{breda_formulation_2012}, with $R_0 = \int_0^\infty A(\tau)\,d\tau$ and $g(\tau) = A(\tau)/R_0$. Analogously, at the individual level, the individual infectiousness profile $a_i(\tau)$ is the product of the individual reproduction number, $\nu_i$, and the individual intrinsic generation interval distribution, $\xi_i(\tau)$. We have  $A(\tau) = E_i[a_i(\tau)]$ \citep{champredon_intrinsic_2015, svensson_note_2007}, and likewise $R_0 = E_i[\nu_i]$ and $g(\tau) = E_i[\xi_i(\tau)]$.

% Both $R_0$ and $g(\tau)$ are expectations over a population. At the individual level, the individual reproduction number $\nu_i$ is the expected number of secondary infections produced by person $i$, with $R_0 = E_i[\nu_i]$ \citep{lloyd-smith_superspreading_2005}. Similarly, the individual infectiousness profile $a_i(\tau)$ describes how person $i$'s infectiousness is distributed over time, with $A(\tau) = E_i[a_i(\tau)]$ \citep{champredon_intrinsic_2015, svensson_note_2007}. The decomposition $a_i(\tau) = \nu_i\,\xi_i(\tau)$ defines an individual intrinsic generation interval distribution $\xi_i(\tau) = a_i(\tau)/\nu_i$, where $\nu_i = \int_0^\infty a_i(\tau)\,d\tau$.

Since $A(\tau)$ is an expectation over individuals, it need not resemble any single person's infectiousness profile. The SEIR model provides a clear illustration: it assumes each individual undergoes a latent period $x_i \sim \text{Exp}(\gamma)$ during which they are not infectious, followed by an infectious period $y_i \sim \text{Exp}(\alpha)$ during which they transmit at rate $\beta$. The resulting ``stepwise'' individual infectiousness profile is
%
\begin{equation}
a_i(\tau) = \begin{cases}
    \beta & \tau \in [x_i, x_i + y_i] \\
    0 & \text{otherwise}
\end{cases}
\label{eq:a_seir_stepwise}
\end{equation}
%
Averaging over individuals yields the population-level infectiousness profile
%
\begin{equation}
A_\text{SEIR}(\tau) = E_i[a_i(\tau)] = \beta \frac{\gamma}{\gamma - \alpha}(e^{-\alpha \tau} - e^{-\gamma \tau})
\label{eq:A_seir}
\end{equation}
% 
--- a smooth, bell-shaped curve that emerges from a collection of discontinuous, rectangular $a_i(\tau)$ ({\bf Figure \ref{fig:a_schematic}A}). Importantly, other choices of $a_i(\tau)$ can produce the same $A_\text{SEIR}(\tau)$. Setting $a_i(\tau) \equiv A_\text{SEIR}(\tau)$ for all individuals gives a ``smooth'' profile in which each person's infectiousness follows the population average ({\bf Figure \ref{fig:a_schematic}B}). At the opposite extreme, $a_i(\tau) = (\beta/\alpha)\,\delta_{\tau_i^*}(\tau)$, with $\tau_i^*$ drawn from $g(\tau) = A_\text{SEIR}(\tau)/R_0$, gives a ``spike'' profile in which each person's infectiousness is concentrated at a single moment ({\bf Figure \ref{fig:a_schematic}C}). All three families --- stepwise, smooth, and spike --- produce the same $A(\tau)$ and hence the same deterministic, mean-field epidemic dynamics.

We have well-established methods for estimating $R_0$ and $g(\tau)$, but not for measuring $a_i(\tau)$. Doing so requires both accurate contact tracing and precise inference of infection times --- data that are rarely available. Viral load kinetics offer an indirect window into the timing of infectiousness, but the mapping is far from straightforward: viral load, viable virus shedding, and infectiousness are distinct quantities. Nevertheless, we can reason about the likely shape of $a_i(\tau)$. The full variability in $g(\tau)$ arises from both within-individual and between-individual variation in infection timing. At one extreme, setting $a_i(\tau) \equiv A(\tau)$ attributes all variability to within-individual variation, but this is unlikely: individuals almost certainly differ in the timing of their peak infectiousness, suggesting that $a_i(\tau)$ is generally narrower than $A(\tau)$. Indeed, some evidence suggests that the window of peak transmissibility for SARS-CoV-2 and influenza may be very short --- less than a day \citep{goyal_viral_2021} --- implying that much of the variation in $g(\tau)$ arises from between-individual differences in when that window occurs.

This raises a natural question: if measuring $a_i(\tau)$ is so challenging, is there any reason to try? Does it add anything beyond knowing $R_0$ and $g(\tau)$? There are several reasons to believe it might. At the individual level, knowing whether one's infectiousness is concentrated in a brief burst or spread over a longer period is important for guiding personal behavior. At the population level, a more punctuated $a_i(\tau)$ could meaningfully alter stochastic epidemic dynamics: individual-level stochastic effects early in an outbreak can cascade through the rest of the epidemic trajectory \citep{morris_computation_2024}. The shape of $a_i(\tau)$ may also interact with control measures that target individual infectiousness, uch as symptom- or test-based screening and isolation, as well as with time-varying contact patterns. The impact of punctuated infectiousness has been examined as a sensitivity analysis in several epidemiological studies \citep{larremore_test_2021} {\bf Others?}, but a systematic understanding of its effect on epidemic dynamics is lacking.

In this paper, we ask: holding $A(\tau)$ constant, how do different choices of $a_i(\tau)$ impact epidemic dynamics? And how would we determine what $a_i(\tau)$ looks like in practice? We focus on the ``punctuatedness'' of $a_i(\tau)$ --- the degree to which an individual's infectiousness is concentrated in time --- as the key parameter of interest. This is equivalent to asking how the decomposition of generation interval variability into within-individual \vs between-individual components affects epidemic behavior. We restrict attention to pathogens with unimodal generation interval distributions and short transmission timescales (\eg, respiratory viruses), though the framework extends in principle to pathogens with more complex dynamics. Interpersonal variation in the overall \emph{level} of infectiousness has been studied extensively \citep{lloyd-smith_superspreading_2005}; here, we focus specifically on interpersonal variation in the \emph{timing} of infectiousness, holding all population-level parameters --- including $R_0$ and $g(\tau)$ --- constant. Our analyses therefore concern stochastic fluctuations around the mean-field solution. We also address the identifiability of the punctuatedness parameter and examine how inferential power changes as a function of $R_0$.
