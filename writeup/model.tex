\section{Expressing the Hudson--Kermack--McKendrick model in terms of individual infectiousness}

\subsection{The individual infectiousness profile}

Early work by Hudson, and later by Kermack and McKendrick \citep{kermack1927}, modeled disease transmission in a population using an integral equation. In modern notation \citep{champredon2015}, the force of infection is
\begin{equation}
F(t) = \int_0^\infty F(t-\tau)\, S(t-\tau)\, A(\tau)\, d\tau
\label{eq:foi}
\end{equation}
where $S(t)$ is the density of susceptible individuals in the population at time $t$, and $A(\tau)$ is the population-level ``infectiousness profile'', a curve describing the expected contribution to the force of infection from an individual who was infected $\tau$ time units ago. The incidence of disease is simply the product of the force of infection and the susceptible density, $F(t) \cdot S(t)$; thus, the infectiousness profile $A(\tau)$ is the fundamental object that determines how an epidemic unfolds.

The infectiousness profile is related to the basic reproduction number, $R_0$, and the generation interval, $g(\tau)$ \citep{champredon2015}:
\begin{equation}
R_0 = \int_0^\infty A(\tau)\, d\tau
\label{eq:R0}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
g(\tau) = \frac{A(\tau)}{R_0}
\label{eq:gi}
\end{equation}

\noindent Furthermore, particular choices of $A(\tau)$ yield disease transmission dynamics that are equivalent to more familiar ordinary differential equation-based models \citep{champredon2015}. For example, the SEIR model
\begin{align}
\frac{dS}{dt} &= -\beta S I \label{eq:seir_s} \\
\frac{dE}{dt} &= \beta S I - \gamma E \label{eq:seir_e} \\
\frac{dI}{dt} &= \gamma E - \alpha I \label{eq:seir_i} \\
\frac{dR}{dt} &= \alpha I \label{eq:seir_r}
\end{align}
is obtained when
\begin{equation}
A(\tau) = \beta \frac{\gamma}{\gamma - \alpha} \left(e^{-\alpha \tau} - e^{-\gamma \tau}\right)
\label{eq:A_seir}
\end{equation}

An individual person's infectiousness kernel $a_i(\tau)$ may differ substantially from $A(\tau)$, and multiple different $a_i(\tau)$ may yield the same $A(\tau)$. For example, the SEIR model is often conceptually motivated by considering an individual who undergoes a latent period of length $X \sim \text{Exp}(\gamma)$ during which they are not infectious, followed by an infectious period of length $Y \sim \text{Exp}(\alpha)$ during which they are infectious at level $\beta$. Mathematically,
\begin{equation}
a_i(\tau) = \begin{cases}
\beta & \quad \tau \in [\tau_i^{\text{on}}, \tau_i^{\text{on}} + \tau_i^{\text{off}}] \\
0 & \quad \text{otherwise}
\end{cases}
\quad \text{where } \tau_i^{\text{on}} \sim \text{Exp}(\gamma) \text{ and } \tau_i^{\text{off}} \sim \text{Exp}(\alpha)
\label{eq:a_stepwise}
\end{equation}

\noindent It can be shown that $E_i [a_i(\tau)] = \lim_{n \rightarrow \infty} \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i = 1}^n a_i(\tau) = A(\tau)$ \citep{champredon2015}. However, other $a_i(\tau)$ also yield the same $A(\tau)$, including
\begin{equation}
a_i(\tau) = \beta \frac{\gamma}{\gamma - \alpha} \left(e^{-\alpha \tau} - e^{-\gamma \tau}\right)
\label{eq:a_smooth}
\end{equation}
where each person's infectiousness profile is a smooth curve that is identical to the population-level infectiousness profile, and
\begin{equation}
a_i(\tau) = \frac{\beta}{\gamma} \cdot \delta_{t_i}(\tau) \qquad \text{where } t_i \text{ is distributed according to the generation interval density, } A(\tau)/R_0
\label{eq:a_spike}
\end{equation}
where each person's infectiousness profile is a delta function, such that all their infectiousness is concentrated at a single moment (\textbf{Figure~XX}).

Like $A(\tau)$, $a_i(\tau)$ is related to the individual reproduction number, $\nu_i$, and the individual generation interval distribution, $\xi_i(\tau)$:
\begin{equation}
\nu_i = \int_0^\infty a_i(\tau)\, d\tau
\label{eq:nu_i}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\xi_i(\tau) = a_i(\tau) / \nu_i
\label{eq:xi_i}
\end{equation}

\subsection{A Gamma convolutional model for the individual infectiousness profile}
\label{sec:gamma_conv}

The ``stepwise'' expression for $a_i(\tau)$ in Eq.~\eqref{eq:a_stepwise} differs from the ``smooth'' and ``spike'' expressions in Eqs.~\eqref{eq:a_smooth}--\eqref{eq:a_spike} in a fundamental way: For Eq.~\eqref{eq:a_stepwise}, $\nu_i$ differs across individuals ($\nu_i \sim \text{XX}$), while for Eqs.~\eqref{eq:a_smooth}--\eqref{eq:a_spike}, $\nu_i$ is uniform across individuals ($\nu_i = R_0$). As a result, the probability of epidemic extinction differs for the stepwise profile relative to both the smooth and spike profiles -- but this is due to an inherent difference in the distribution of the individual reproduction number, not to the timing of the infections. Because of this fundamental dissimilarity---and because the impact of variation in $\nu_i$ on epidemic dynamics has been discussed extensively elsewhere \citep{lloydsmith2005}---we will focus most of the manuscript on individual infectiousness profiles of the type in Eqs.~\eqref{eq:a_smooth}--\eqref{eq:a_spike}, where the individual infectiousness $\nu_i$ is uniform across the population. That way, the impact of the timing of infectiousness can be examined in isolation.

To achieve this, we seek a family of functions that allow us to model individual infectiousness profiles with the following characteristics:
\begin{itemize}
    \item All have uniform total infectiousness: $\int_0^\infty a_i(\tau)\, d\tau = R_0$
    \item Smooth, single-peaked distributions
    \item All have identical shape, except for a time shift (no mixtures of broad smooth distributions with narrow spiky distributions)
\end{itemize}

A candidate set of functions that obeys this is the set of Gamma convolutional curves. Let $A(\tau)$ be Gamma distributed (up to a scaling constant):
\begin{equation}
A(\tau) = R_0\, f_A(\tau) \qquad \text{where } f_A(\tau) \sim \text{Gamma}(\alpha, r), \quad r = \alpha / \mu
\label{eq:A_gamma}
\end{equation}
where $\mu$ is the mean generation interval and $\alpha$ controls the shape of the population-level infectiousness profile.

Now, let
\begin{equation}
a_i(\tau) = R_0\, f_a(\tau - s_i) \qquad \text{where } f_a(\tau) \sim \text{Gamma}(\kappa, r) \quad \text{and} \quad s_i \sim \text{Gamma}(\alpha - \kappa, r)
\label{eq:a_gamma}
\end{equation}

Here, $\kappa \in (0, \alpha)$ is a parameter governing the punctuatedness of $a_i(\tau)$, with smaller $\kappa$ yielding a more concentrated infectiousness profile (\textbf{Figure~XX}). Since the sum of two Gamma-distributed random variables with the same rate is also Gamma distributed (with the same rate and with shape equal to the sum of the two component shapes), this formulation is guaranteed to converge to $A(\tau)$ in expectation (\textbf{Supplementary Methods}). Furthermore, the $\kappa$-values may differ across individuals; regardless of the distribution they are drawn from, the Gamma sum property ensures that $E_i[a_i(\tau)] = A(\tau)$ (\textbf{Supplementary Methods}, \textbf{Figure~XX}).


\subsection{Splitting the infectiousness profile into biological infectiousness and contacts}

From Eqs.~\eqref{eq:nu_i}--\eqref{eq:xi_i}, the individual infectiousness profile can be expressed in terms of the individual generation interval distribution and the individual reproduction number:
\begin{equation}
a_i(\tau) = \xi_i(\tau)\, \nu_i
\label{eq:a_factored}
\end{equation}

A different and more general decomposition is also possible, into factors relating to a person's ``biological infectiousness'', $b_i(\tau)$, and ``contact potential'', $c_i(t)$:
\begin{equation}
a_i(\tau) = b_i(\tau)\, c_i(t_i + \tau)
\label{eq:a_decomp}
\end{equation}

The biological infectiousness, $b_i(\tau)$, is a nonnegative density function that integrates to 1 ($\int_0^\infty b_i(\tau)\, d\tau = 1$) that describes how a person's potential infectiousness is spread over time. The contact potential, $c_i(t)$, is a nonnegative time-varying function that describes the expected number of individuals person $i$ would infect if all their infectiousness were concentrated at the single moment $t$ (i.e., if $b_i(\tau)$ were a delta function). Thus, $c_i(t)$ incorporates both a person's contact rate and the probability of transmission given contact. It can immediately be seen that if $c_i(t)$ is constant, then we recover Eq.~\eqref{eq:a_factored} where $b_i(\tau)$ is the individual generation interval distribution and $c_i(t) = \nu_i$. For time-varying $c_i(t)$, this form shows that the individual reproduction number and the individual generation interval distribution depend not just on the time since infection $\tau$, but also on clock time $t$. For infection time $t^*$, we obtain %CHECK THESE
\begin{equation}
\nu_i(t^*) = \int_0^{\infty} b_i(\tau)\, c_i(t^* + \tau)\, d\tau
\label{eq:nu_tv}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\xi_i(t^*, \tau) = \frac{b_i(\tau)\, c_i(t^* + \tau)}{\nu_i(t^*)}
\label{eq:xi_tv}
\end{equation}

This decomposition feeds back into the population-level model. If we assume that $b_i$ and $c_i$ are independent (i.e., an individual's biological infectiousness is independent of their contact pattern), then
\begin{equation}
A(\tau) = E[a_i(\tau)] = B(\tau) \cdot C(t_i + \tau)
\label{eq:A_decomp}
\end{equation}
where $B(\tau) = E_i[b_i(\tau)]$ and $C(t_i + \tau) = E_i[c_i(t + \tau)]$. Likewise, if $C$ is constant in time, this reduces to the familiar
\begin{equation}
A(\tau) = g(\tau)\, R_0
\label{eq:A_simple}
\end{equation}

Splitting $a_i(\tau)$ into biological infectiousness $b_i$ and contact potential $c_i$ allows us to separately consider how the shape of a person's infectiousness, unconditional on their contact patterns, impacts transmission dynamics under various contact patterns.

For the Gamma convolutional model, we have
\begin{equation}
b_i(\tau) = f_a(\tau - s_i)
\label{eq:b_gamma}
\end{equation}
and
\begin{equation}
c_i(t) = R_0 \quad \text{for all } i, t
\label{eq:c_gamma}
\end{equation}
