\section{Modeling individual infectiousness}

\subsection{The time-shifted Gamma model}

While our interest is in the general question of how $a_i(\tau)$, independent of $A(\tau)$, impacts epidemic dynamics, we introduce here a specific modeling framework --- general enough to capture the essential features --- that allows us to make precise claims. We seek a definition of $a_i(\tau)$ satisfying three requirements: (i) $A(\tau) = E_i[a_i(\tau)]$, as required by definition; (ii) $\nu_i = R_0$ for all $i$, so that there is no inherent interpersonal variation in total infectiousness; and (iii) the width of $a_i(\tau)$ is governed by a single smoothness parameter $\psi \in [0,1]$, where $\psi = 1$ yields $a_i(\tau) \equiv A(\tau)$ (the smooth case, in which each person's individual infectiousness profile matches the population average) and $\psi \to 0$ makes $a_i(\tau)$ a delta function (the spike case, in which all of a person's secondary infections occur at a single moment).

We assume the intrinsic generation interval distribution $g(\tau)$ follows a $\text{Gamma}(\alpha, \beta)$ distribution with $\beta = \alpha/\bar{g}$, where $\bar{g}$ is the mean generation time --- a common choice in applied epidemiology. Define each person's individual generation interval distribution as
%
\begin{equation}
    \xi_i(\tau) = \begin{cases}
      0 & \tau \leq l_i \\
      f_\epsilon(\tau - l_i) & \tau > l_i
    \end{cases}
    \label{eq:gamma_timeshift}
\end{equation}
%
where $l_i \sim \text{Gamma}((1 - \psi)\alpha, \beta)$ is a random latent period and $f_\epsilon$ is the $\text{Gamma}(\psi\alpha, \beta)$ density. Setting $a_i(\tau) = R_0\,\xi_i(\tau)$ immediately satisfies requirement~(ii). For requirement~(iii), as $\psi \to 1$ the latent period vanishes ($l_i \to 0$) and $f_\epsilon$ approaches the full $\text{Gamma}(\alpha, \beta)$ density, so $\xi_i(\tau) = g(\tau)$ for all $i$; as $\psi \to 0$, $f_\epsilon$ concentrates to a point mass and $\xi_i(\tau)$ becomes a delta function at $\tau = l_i$.

For requirement~(i), observe that the time from infection to a secondary infection attempt is $\tau = l_i + U_i$, where $U_i \sim \text{Gamma}(\psi\alpha, \beta)$ is the draw from $f_\epsilon$. Since $l_i$ and $U_i$ are independent Gamma random variables with a common rate $\beta$, their sum follows a $\text{Gamma}((1-\psi)\alpha + \psi\alpha, \beta) = \text{Gamma}(\alpha, \beta)$ distribution by the additive property of the Gamma family. Taking expectations over individuals, $E_i[\xi_i(\tau)] = g(\tau)$, so $E_i[a_i(\tau)] = R_0\,g(\tau) = A(\tau)$ as required. Notably, this convergence does not require the population to share a common value of $\psi$: individual-specific values $\psi_i$ drawn from any distribution on $[0,1]$ still yield $E_i[a_i(\tau)] = A(\tau)$ ({\bf Supplementary Methods}).

\subsection{Allowing for time-varying contacts}

The formulation above assumes time-homogeneous contacts, which is what allows $a_i(\tau)$ and $A(\tau)$ to depend only on time since infection $\tau$ and not on calendar time $t$. We follow this convention for most of our analyses. However, the interaction between punctuated infectiousness and time-varying contacts turns out to be consequential, so we briefly introduce the relevant generalization and two contact models that serve as test beds.

Under time-uniform contacts, $a_i(\tau) = R_0\,\xi_i(\tau)$, where $R_0$ and $\xi_i(\tau)$ are defined with respect to a reference contact process $\bar{c} \equiv 1$. To allow for time-varying contacts, we introduce a contact modulator $c_i(t)$, a nonnegative function describing person $i$'s proportional deviation from the reference contact level at calendar time $t$. We require $E_t[c_i(t)] = 1$, so that $R_0$ retains its interpretation as the expected number of secondary infections under average contact conditions; this is satisfied by any periodic function with mean~1 or any stationary random process with mean~1. The individual infectiousness profile generalizes to
%
\begin{equation}
    a_i(t_i, \tau) = R_0\,\xi_i(\tau)\,c_i(t_i + \tau)
    \label{eq:ai_contact}
\end{equation}
%
where $t_i$ is person $i$'s infection time. This expression makes explicit that, when contacts vary, a person's realized infectiousness depends not only on their biological profile $\xi_i(\tau)$ but also on when they happen to be infectious. From Eq.~\ref{eq:ai_contact}, we can define the realized individual reproduction number,
%
\begin{equation}
    \nu_i^\text{rel}(t_i) = R_0 \int_0^\infty \xi_i(\tau)\,c_i(t_i + \tau)\,d\tau
    \label{eq:nu_rel}
\end{equation}
%
and the realized individual generation interval distribution,
%
\begin{equation}
    \xi_i^\text{rel}(t_i, \tau) = \frac{\xi_i(\tau)\,c_i(t_i + \tau)}{\displaystyle\int_0^\infty \xi_i(\tau')\,c_i(t_i + \tau')\,d\tau'}
    \label{eq:xi_rel}
\end{equation}
%
so that $a_i(t_i, \tau) = \nu_i^\text{rel}(t_i)\,\xi_i^\text{rel}(t_i, \tau)$. When contacts are time-uniform ($c_i \equiv 1$), these reduce to $\nu_i^\text{rel} = R_0$ and $\xi_i^\text{rel} = \xi_i$. When contacts vary, $\nu_i^\text{rel}$ fluctuates around $R_0$: a person whose brief window of infectiousness coincides with a period of high contact activity will have $\nu_i^\text{rel} > R_0$, while one whose window falls during a low-contact period will have $\nu_i^\text{rel} < R_0$. Crucially, this effect is stronger when $\xi_i(\tau)$ is narrow (small $\psi$), because a concentrated profile effectively samples the contact process at a single point, whereas a broad profile averages over it.

As test cases, we consider two models. For the \emph{periodic} contact model, we set
%
\begin{equation}
    c_i(t) = 1 - A\cos(\omega t) \quad \forall\,i
    \label{eq:periodic_c}
\end{equation}
%
with amplitude $A \in [0,1]$ and angular frequency $\omega > 0$. The default $\omega = 2\pi\,\text{day}^{-1}$ models diurnal contact variation, with higher contact rates during the day and lower rates at night. Setting $A = 0$ recovers the reference process. In this model, all individuals share the same contact trajectory, so there is temporal variation within each person's contacts but no variation across individuals.

For the \emph{stochastic} contact model, each person's contact modulator is a piecewise-constant process that switches to a new level at the points of a Poisson process with rate $\lambda$, so that sojourn times at each level are exponentially distributed with mean $1/\lambda$. The levels are drawn independently from a $\text{Gamma}(k_c,\, k_c)$ distribution,
%
\begin{equation}
    f_{c^*}(c) = \frac{k_c^{k_c}}{\Gamma(k_c)}\,c^{k_c - 1}\,e^{-k_c\,c}, \qquad c \geq 0
    \label{eq:gamma_contact_pdf}
\end{equation}
%
which has mean~1 (satisfying the reference-process constraint) and variance $1/k_c$, leaving a one-parameter family indexed by the \emph{contact dispersion} $k_c > 0$. Smaller $k_c$ yields more heterogeneous contacts: for $k_c < 1$ the density is L-shaped, producing many quiet periods punctuated by occasional bursts of high contact activity; for $k_c > 1$ the density is bell-shaped and increasingly concentrated around~1; and as $k_c \to \infty$ the distribution collapses to a point mass at~1, recovering the constant-contact reference process. In contrast to the periodic model, each person's contact trajectory is unique, but the population average is constant: $C(t) = E_i[c_i(t)] = 1$.

Depending on the application, either the periodic or the stochastic contact model may more readily yield to analytical treatment; but we present simulation-based results for both models whenever time-varying contacts are considered.

\subsection{Simulation approach} 

We investigate the impact of punctuatedness on epidemic dynamics using both closed-form analytic results (derived in the {\bf Supplementary Methods}) and stochastic simulations. Stochastic simulations use a branching-process-like algorithm in a finite population of $N = 10{,}000$ susceptible individuals with a single initial infectious individual. For each infected person $i$:
%
\begin{enumerate}
    \item A latent period $l_i$ is drawn from $\text{Gamma}((1-\psi)\alpha, \beta)$, defining the individual generation interval distribution $\xi_i(\tau)$ via Eq.~\ref{eq:gamma_timeshift}. When contacts are non-uniform, a contact trajectory $c_i(t)$ is also drawn from the relevant contact model.
    \item The number of secondary infection attempts is drawn as $X_i \sim \text{Poisson}(R_0)$.
    \item The timing of each attempt relative to infection is drawn from $\xi_i(\tau)$.
    \item When the contact process is non-uniform ($c_i \neq 1$), Poisson thinning is applied: each attempt at calendar time $t_i + \tau$ is accepted with probability proportional to $c_i(t_i + \tau)$, ensuring that the realized infection rate matches $a_i(t_i, \tau)$.
    \item Each accepted attempt is paired with a uniformly chosen individual from the population: if the recipient is susceptible, infection occurs; otherwise, the attempt fails.
\end{enumerate}
%
Full details are provided in the {\bf Supplementary Methods}. For illustration, we focus on three sets of epidemiological parameters roughly pertaining to influenza-like, SARS-CoV-2 omicron-like, and measles-like pathogens ({\bf Table XX}).
