\section{The individual infectiousness profile}
\subsection{Expressing the Hudson--Kermack--McKendrick model in terms of individual infectiousness}

Early work by Hudson, and later by Kermack and McKendrick \citep{kermack_contribution_1927}, modeled the transmission of a permanently immunizing infection using an integral equation. In modern notation \citep{breda_formulation_2012}, the force of infection is
%
\begin{equation} 
F(t) = \int_0^\infty F(t - \tau) S(t - \tau) A(\tau) d\tau 
\label{eq:foi}
\end{equation}
%
where $F(t)$ is the force of infection at time $t$, $S(t)$ is the density of susceptible individuals in the population at time $t$, and $A(\tau)$ is the infectiousness profile describing the expected contribution to the force of infection from an individual who was infected $\tau$ time units ago. The incidence of disease is the product of the force of infection and the susceptible density (\ie, $-\frac{dS}{dt} = F(t) S(t)$); thus, the infectiousness profile $A(\tau)$ is the fundamental object that determines how an epidemic unfolds. 

The infectiousness profile is related to the basic reproduction number $R_0$ and the generation interval distribution $g(\tau)$: 
%
\begin{equation}
R_0 = \int_0^\infty A(\tau) d\tau
\label{eq:R0}
\end{equation}
%
\begin{equation}
g(\tau) = \frac{A(\tau)}{R_0}
\label{eq:gi}
\end{equation}

Since $A(\tau)$ is an expectation over individuals, it does not necessarily reflect any single person's infectiousness. For example: the SEIR model is conceptually motivated by considering an individual who undergoes a latent period of length $x_i \sim \text{Exp}(\gamma)$ during which they are not infectious, followed by an infectious period of length $y_i \sim \text{Exp}(\alpha)$ during which they are infectious at level $\beta$. Mathematically, this ``stepwise'' individual infectiousness profile is expressed as 
%
\begin{equation}
a_i(\tau) = \begin{cases}
    \beta & \tau \in [x_i, x_i + y_i] \\ 
    0 & \text{otherwise}
\end{cases}
\label{eq:a_seir_stepwise}
\end{equation}
%
This yields the population-level infectiousness kernel 
%
\begin{equation}
A(\tau) = E_i[a_i(\tau)] = \lim_{n \rightarrow \infty} \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n a_i(\tau) = \beta \frac{\gamma}{\gamma - \alpha}(e^{-\alpha \tau} - e^{-\gamma \tau})
\label{eq:A_seir}
\end{equation}
%
\ie, a smooth, bell-shaped curve that arises from a sum of discontinuous, stepwise $a_i(\tau)$ ({\bf Figure XX}). Other choices of $a_i(\tau)$ can yield the same $A(\tau)$ in Eq.~\ref{eq:A_seir}, including 
%
\begin{equation}
a_i(\tau) = \beta \frac{\gamma}{\gamma - \alpha} (e^{-\alpha \tau} - e^{-\gamma \tau})
\label{eq:a_seir_smooth}
\end{equation}
%
\ie, a ``smooth'' individual infectiousness profile where each person's infectiousness is identical to the population-level average, and 
%
\begin{equation}
a_i(\tau) = \frac{\beta}{\gamma} \delta_{t_i}(\tau)
\label{eq:a_seir_spike}
\end{equation}
%
\ie, a ``spike'' infectiousness profile where $\delta_i(\tau)$ is a delta function with mass at time $t_i$, and $t_i$ is distributed according to the generation interval density $g(\tau) = A(\tau) / R_0$, such that a person's infectiousness is concentrated at a single moment ({\bf Figure \ref{fig:seir_sims} A--C}).

In general, any definition of $a_i(\tau)$ such that $E_i[a_i(\tau)] = A(\tau)$ will yield the same mean-field population-level epidemic dynamics; however, different choices of $a_i(\tau)$ may have important stochastic consequences ({\bf Figure \ref{fig:seir_sims} D--I}), which are the main topic of this article.

\subsection{Properties and components of the individual infectiousness profile}

Like $A(\tau)$, $a_i(\tau)$ is related to the individual reproduction number $\nu_i$ and the individual intrinsic generation interval distribution $\xi_i(\tau)$ (compare Eqs.~\ref{eq:R0}--\ref{eq:gi}): 
%
\begin{equation}
    \nu_i = \int_0^\infty a_i(\tau) d\tau 
    \label{eq:nu_def}
\end{equation}
%
\begin{equation}
    \xi_i(\tau) = \frac{a_i(\tau)}{\nu_i}
    \label{eq:indgi_def}
\end{equation}
%
Re-arranging Eq.~\ref{eq:indgi_def}, 
%
\begin{equation}
    a_i(\tau) = \nu_i \xi_i(\tau)
    \label{eq:ai_expression}
\end{equation}
%
which is a decomposition of $a_i(\tau)$ into infection intensity ($\nu_i$) and timing ($\xi_i(\tau)$). This decomposition assumes that interpersonal contacts are uniform over time --- or else that any non-uniformity is captured implicitly in $\nu_i$ and/or $\xi_i(\tau)$. A futher decomposition is possible by introducing an explicit contact process $c_i(t)$, defined with respect to the reference contact process $\bar{c}$ against which both $R_0$ and $\nu_i$ are defined \citep{dietz_estimation_1993}. Let $c_i(t)$ be a nonnegative function describing person $i$'s proportional deviations from $\bar{c}$ (so $c_i(t) \equiv 1$ represents the reference process), and let $b_i(\tau)$ be a density capturing the timing of person $i$'s secondary infection attempts under $\bar{c}$. Then, we can define
%
\begin{equation}
    a_i(t_i, \tau) = \nu_i b_i(\tau) c_i(t_i + \tau) 
    \label{eq:abc}
\end{equation}
%
where $t_i$ is person $i$'s infection time and $\tau$ represents time since infection. For $\nu_i$ to retain its meaning, we require $E_t[c_i(t)] = 1$, that is, for a randomly chosen time $t$, the expected value of person $i$'s contact modulator is 1. This is satisfied, for example, by a periodic function with average value 1 or by a stationary random process with mean 1. Another way of interpreting the contact modulator is that $\nu_i c_i(t)$ is the expected number of people that person $i$ would infect if all their infectiousness were concentrated at time $t$ (\ie, if $b_i$ were a delta function with mass at time $t$). The density $b_i(\tau)$ therefore reflects the ``biological'' component of a person's infectiousness timing (\ie, the timing of secondary infections under a time-uniform reference contact process), and $c_i(t)$ captures the ``contact-related'' component of a person's infectiousness timing, while $\nu_i$ modulates the person's total infectiousness, which may be composed of both biological (\eg, greater viral shedding) and contact-related (\eg, persons with many social contacts) factors. 

This extends back to the population-level model. If we assume that $c_i$ is independent of $\nu_i$ and $b_i$, but allow $\nu_i$ and $b_i$ to depend on one another (for example, we might expect a person with high overall infectiousness to also have a longer duration of infectiousness), then the population-level infectiousness profile is 
%
\begin{equation}
    A(t_i, \tau) = E_i[a_i(\tau)] = E_i[\nu_i b_i(\tau)] E_i[c_i(t_i + \tau)]
    \label{eq:A_def_general}
\end{equation}
%
with the expectation taken over the population indexed by $i$. We define $C(t) = E_i[c_i(t)]$ to be the population-mean contact modulator at calendar time $t$. We define $B(\tau) = E_i\Bigl[b_i(\tau) \frac{\nu_i}{R_0}\Bigr]$, \ie, the population-average biological infection timing density weighted by the person's infectiousness relative to the population mean. With these definitions, we obtain 
%
\begin{equation}
    A(t_i, \tau) = R_0 B(\tau) C(t_i+\tau)
    \label{eq:ABC}
\end{equation}
%
Note that, like $c_i(t)$, $C(t)$ has time-average 1, since $E_t[C(t)] = E_t[E_i[c_i(t)]] = E_i[E_t[c_i(t)]] = 1$ (Fubini). If $C(t) \equiv 1$, we recover the original expression of $A(\tau) = R_0 B(\tau) = R_0 g(\tau)$, where $B(\tau)$ becomes the intrinsic generation interval distribution.

\subsection{A time-shifted Gamma model for biological infectiousness profiles with arbitrary punctuation}

Our central goal is to examine how the shape of the individual infectiousness profile $a_i(\tau)$ impacts epidemic dynamics, holding all else (including $R_0$ and $g(\tau)$) constant. The ``stepwise'' expression for $a_i(\tau)$ in Eq.~\ref{eq:a_seir_stepwise} poses a difficulty: it introduces inherent overdispersion in the secondary infection distribution ($\nu_i = \int_0^\infty a_i(\tau) d\tau \sim XX$), while for the smooth and spike profiles, $\nu_i$ is uniform across individuals ($\nu_i = R_0 \forall i$). This overdispersion affects stochastic epidemic dynamics, including the probability of epidemic establishment \citep{lloyd-smith_superspreading_2005}. The impact of overdispersion on stochastic epidemic dynamics has been studied extensively elsewhere \citep{lloyd-smith_superspreading_2005}. Here, we seek a family of functions that do not feature this inherent overdispersion, using the smooth and spike infectiousness profiles as book-ends. 

Let us constrain the generation interval distribution $g(\tau)$ to be unimodal, and let us seek a family of functions $b_i(\tau)$ that are also unimodal with the narrowness of the function (variance of the distribution) modulated by a parameter $\psi \in [0,1]$, where $\psi \rightarrow 0$ yields a delta function (the spike case) and $\psi \rightarrow 1$ makes $b_i(\tau) \equiv g(\tau) \forall i$ (the smooth case). If we assume $g(\tau)$ is Gamma-distributed, which is a common choice for modeling generation interval distributions, the setup is straightforward. Specifically, let 
%
\begin{equation}
    g(\tau) \sim \text{Gamma}(\alpha, \beta), \qquad \beta = \alpha / \mu 
    \label{eq:gamma_gi}
\end{equation}
%
where $\mu$ is the mean generation time, and $\alpha$ controls the shape of the generation interval distribution. A key property of the Gamma distribution is that the sum of two Gamma-distributed random variables with shape parameters $\alpha_1$ and $\alpha_2$ and common rate $\beta$ is also Gamma-distributed with shape $\alpha_1 + \alpha_2$ and rate $\beta$. Let us assume now that the individual-level biological infectiousness profile $b_i(\tau)$ follows a shifted Gamma distribution preceded by a latent period $l_i$, which is also Gamma-distributed, \ie: 
%
\begin{equation}
    b_i(\tau) = \begin{cases}
      0 & \tau \leq l_i  \\ 
      f_\epsilon(\tau) & \tau > l_i 
    \end{cases}
    \label{eq:gamma_timeshift}
\end{equation}
% 
where $l_i \sim \text{Gamma}((1-\psi)\alpha, \beta)$ and $f_\epsilon(\tau)$ is a $\text{Gamma}(\psi \alpha, \beta)$ pdf. The parameter $\psi \in [0,1]$ determines the punctuatedness of $b_i(\tau)$, with $\psi \rightarrow 0$ making $b_i(\tau)$ a delta function concentrated at $\tau = l_i$, and $\psi \rightarrow 1$ making $b_i(\tau) = g(\tau)$. As such, this provides a continuum between the ``delta'' and ``smooth'' cases from before (Eqs.~\ref{eq:a_seir_smooth}-\ref{eq:a_seir_spike}). Due to the additive property of Gamma distributions with equal rate, these individual-level profiles are guaranteed to converge to the population-level $g(\tau) \sim \text{Gamma}(\alpha,\beta)$. Furthermore, this convergence does not depend on the entire population sharing the same $\psi$-value; indeed, unique $\psi_i$ may be chosen from any distribution with support on $[0,1]$ and the convergence holds ({\bf Supplementary Methods}).

\subsection{Analytic approach}

To investigate the impact of the punctuatedness of $a_i(\tau)$ on epidemic dynamics, we turn to both analytic results and stochastic simultions. Stochastic simulations are generated by assuming a fixed population size (10,000) and initial number of infectious individuals (1). For each person $i$, a biological and contact infectiousness potential ($b_i$ and $c_i$), and thus the full individual infectiousness profile ($a_i$), are generated {\it a priori}. Infection attempts are generated for each person by drawing a number of infections $X \sim \text{Poisson}(\nu_i)$, where $\nu_i = \int_0^\infty a_i(\tau) d\tau$. These infections are then distributed according to $a_i(\tau)$ using Gamma-distributed draws. Infection attempts are paired with ``recipients'' uniform-randomly: if the recipient is susceptible, infection succeeds, while if the person has already been infected, the infection attempt fails. Full deatils are given in the {\bf Supplementary Methods}. For illustration purposes, we focus on three sets of epidemiological parameters, roughly pertaining to influenza-like, SARS-CoV-2 omicron-like, and measles-like pathogens ({\bf Table XX}). 

We normally keep the contact process fixed at the reference value ($c_i(t) \equiv 1$). However, to examine the interaction between $b_i(\tau)$ and $c_i(t)$, we introduce a ``periodic'' and a ``Pareto'' contact model. 

For the periodic contact model, we assume 
% 
\begin{equation}
    c_i(t) = 1 - \epsilon \cos(2 \pi t) \forall i 
\end{equation}
%
This models a contact process with a period of one day, reflecting higher numbers of contacts during the day and lower numbers at night. The amplitude of the contact process is governed by the parameter $\epsilon \in [0,1]$, with $\epsilon=0$ recovering the reference contact proces $c_i(\tau) \equiv 1$. Note that this contact process also yields a periodic population-level contact modulator: $E_i[c_i(t)] = 1 - \epsilon \cos(2 \pi t)$. Here, there is no variation between individuals' contact processes, but there is variation over time. 

For the Pareto contact model, we assume each person's contact modulator switches to a new level at times $t^* \sim \text{Exp}(\lambda)$, \ie, according to a Poisson process. The levels that are switched to are independently drawn from a $c^*\sim \text{Pareto}(xx,xx)$ distribution, which has mean 1. For this contact process, each person's infectiousness profile is unique, but the population-average infectiousness profile $C(t) = E_i[c_i(t)] = 1$, meaning the population-level, mean-field dynamics follow the reference contact process.

Since the sum of two Gamma-distributed random variables with the same rate is also Gamma distributed (with the same rate and with shape equal to the sum of the two component shapes), this formulation is guaranteed to converge to $A(\tau)$ in expectation (\textbf{Supplementary Methods}). Furthermore, the $\psi$-values may differ across individuals; regardless of the distribution they are drawn from, the Gamma sum property ensures that $E_i[a_i(\tau)] = A(\tau)$ (\textbf{Supplementary Methods}, \textbf{Figure~XX}).
