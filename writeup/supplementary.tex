\subsubsection*{Parameter structure}

The punctuated Gamma model has four epidemiological parameters: the basic  reproduction number $R_0$, the Malthusian growth rate $r$, and the two  parameters of the generation interval distribution, $\alpha$ (shape) and  $\beta$ (rate), which together determine the mean generation time  $\bar{g} = \alpha/\beta$. These four parameters are not all free: the  Euler--Lotka equation,
%
\begin{equation}
    R_0 \left(\frac{\beta}{\beta + r}\right)^\alpha = 1,
    \label{eq:euler_lotka}
\end{equation}
%
provides one constraint among them, leaving three free parameters. We  choose to work with $(R_0, \bar{g}, \alpha)$ as the free parameters,  since each has a direct epidemiological interpretation:
%
\begin{itemize}
    \item $R_0$: the basic reproduction number, measuring transmissibility;
    \item $\bar{g}$: the mean generation time;
    \item $\alpha$: the shape parameter of the generation interval  distribution, where larger $\alpha$ corresponds to a more concentrated  (less variable) generation interval for fixed $\bar{g}$.
\end{itemize}
%
Given $(R_0, \bar{g}, \alpha)$, the remaining parameters are fully  determined. The rate parameter $\beta$ follows immediately from the  definition of the mean:
%
\begin{equation}
    \beta = \frac{\alpha}{\bar{g}},
\end{equation}
%
and the Malthusian growth rate $r$ follows from the Euler--Lotka equation:
%
\begin{equation}
    r = \beta\left(R_0^{1/\alpha} - 1\right) 
      = \frac{\alpha}{\bar{g}}\left(R_0^{1/\alpha} - 1\right).
    \label{eq:r_from_params}
\end{equation}
%
The dimensionless growth parameter $z = r/\beta = r\bar{g}/\alpha$ is 
therefore also determined:
%
\begin{equation}
    z = R_0^{1/\alpha} - 1,
    \label{eq:z_from_R0}
\end{equation}
%
confirming that $z$ depends on $R_0$ and $\alpha$ alone. The punctuation  parameter $\psi \in [0, 1]$ is the only remaining free parameter, and  it is the sole quantity that is not identifiable from population-level  epidemic data: since $A(\tau)$ is invariant across $\psi$ by  construction, the parameters $(R_0, \bar{g}, \alpha)$ can in principle  be estimated from the observed epidemic trajectory independently of  $\psi$. The consequences of varying $\psi$ while holding  $(R_0, \bar{g}, \alpha)$ fixed are therefore attributable purely to  changes in the individual-level temporal structure of infectiousness,  with no confounding from changes in the population-level transmission  kernel.

For the analyses presented here, we fix $(R_0, \bar{g}, \alpha)$ and  vary $\psi \in [0, 1]$, computing $\text{Var}(W)$ and  $\text{Mode}[\varsigma]$ across this range. Representative parameter  values are chosen to reflect pathogens of public health interest,  with $\alpha$ and $\bar{g}$ matched to empirical generation interval  estimates where available.

\subsubsection*{Derivation of $\text{Var}[W]$}

Branching process theory establishes that, for a branching process $Z(t)$ conditional on non-extinction, $e^{-rt} Z(t) \rightarrow W$ almost surely as $t \rightarrow \infty$. Here, $r$ is the early-epidemic exponential growth rate and $W$ is a random variable that effectively scales the branching process' initial condition, capturing stochastic fluctuations in the early-epidemic process. 

With a change of variables, we can translate $W$ into a random initial time shift: 
%
\begin{equation}
	\varsigma = \frac{\log W - \log E[W]}{r} = \frac{\log W}{r}
\end{equation}
%
when $I_0 = E[W] = 1$. Our goal is to understand how $\text{Var}[\varsigma]$ depends on the punctuation parameter $\psi$. Since $\varsigma$ and $W$ are monotonically related, we can derive an expression for $\text{Var}[W]$ and will interpret its implications for $\text{Var}[\varsigma]$ at the end. 

To start, we consider an index case $i$ infected at time $t = 0$ who produces $\chi_i \sim \text{Poisson}(R_0)$ offspring. Under the Gamma time-shift model, the offspring $j$ from this index case are infected at times $\tau_j = l_i + \epsilon_j$, where $l_i$ is person $i$'s latent period and $\epsilon_j$ is the additional lag between $l_i$ and person $j$'s infection time, with 
%
\[l_i \sim \text{Gamma}((1-\psi)\alpha, \beta), \qquad \epsilon_j \sim \text{Gamma}(\psi \alpha, \beta) \]
%
This ensures that the population-level generation interval distribution is
%
\[ g(\tau) \sim \text{Gamma}(\alpha, \beta) \]
%
We can define some useful quantities that will arise later in the derivation. First, from the Euler-Lotka equation under $g(\tau)$ \citep{wallinga_how_2007}:, 
%
\[1 = R_0 \int_{\tau=0}^\infty e^{-r\tau } \frac{\beta^\alpha}{\Gamma(\alpha)} \tau^{\alpha-1} e^{-\beta \tau} d\tau\]
%
which simplifies to 
%
\[ 1 = R_0 \Bigl( \frac{\beta}{\beta+r }\Bigr )^\alpha \]
%
% Solving for $r$ gives 
%
% \[ r = \beta (R_0^{1/\alpha} - 1) \]
%
We define 
%
\[ \rho = \frac{\beta}{\beta + r}, \qquad \rho_2 = \frac{\beta}{\beta + 2r} \]
%
This allows us to re-write the Euler-Lotka equation: 
%
\[ 1 = R_0 \rho^\alpha \]

Next, we introduce the distributional fixed point equation, which is a standard starting point for computing moments of $W$. This equation exploits the self-similarity of the branching process, in which an epidemic initiated by a single individual is, in distribution, a scaled and time-shifted copy of the whole epidemic. At large $t$, the epidemic seeded by the index case $i$ consists of the downstream epidemics caused by their offspring $j$, which have grown approximately to size $Z_j(t) = W_j e^{r(t - \tau_j)}$. That is, 
%
\[
	Z(t) \approx \sum_{j=1}^{\chi_i} W_j e^{rt} e^{-r \tau_j}
\]
%
% Consider an epidemic seeded by a single index case $i$ at time $t = 0$. This person produces $\chi_i \sim \text{Poisson}(R_0)$ offspring at times $\tau_1, \tau_2, \dots, \tau_{\chi_i}$, measured from person $i$'s infection time ($\tau = 0$). Each offspring $j$, infected at time $\tau_j$, then seeds their own independent sub-epidemic. 
% Now, we ask: what does the epidemic look like at large $t$? The epidemic seeded by index case $i$ consists of the downstream epidemics caused by their offspring $j$. At time $t$, these offspring epidemics have grown approximately to size $Z_j(t) = e^{r(t-\tau_j)} W_j $. In other words, 
% %
% \[Z(t) \approx \sum_{j=1}^{\chi_i} e^{rt} e^{-r \tau_j}\]
% % 
Multiplying through by $e^{-rt}$: 
%
\[  W \overset{d}{=} \sum_{j=1}^{\chi_i} e^{-r \tau_j} W_j \]

We now use this expression to calculate $\text{Var}[W]$. Since randomness enters through both $W$ and $\tau$, we first condition on $\tau_j$ using the law of total variance: 
%
\[ \text{Var}[W] = E[\text{Var}\Bigl[\sum_j e^{-r \tau_j} W_j | \tau_j\Bigr]] + \text{Var}[E\Bigl[\sum_j e^{-r \tau_j} W_j | \tau_j\Bigr]] \]
%
Given $\tau_j$, $W_j$ are i.i.d.~with $E[W] = 1$ and variance $\text{Var}[W]$, so 
%
\[
= E[\text{Var}\Bigl[W \sum_j e^{-r \tau_j} \Bigr]]  + 
\text{Var}[E\Bigl[W \sum_j e^{-r \tau_j}\Bigr]]
\]
\[
=E\Bigl[\sum_j e^{-2r\tau_j} \cdot \text{Var}[W]\Bigr] + 
\text{Var}\Bigl[E[W] \cdot E[\sum_j e^{-r \tau_j}]\Bigr]
\]
\[
= E\Bigl[\sum_j e^{-2 r \tau_j}\Bigr] \cdot \text{Var}[W] + 
(E[W])^2 \cdot \text{Var}\Bigl[\sum_j e^{-r \tau_j}\Bigr]
\]
%
(a little sloppy, clean this up, but we get to the right place). \\
Since $E[W] = I_0 = 1$, we have 
\[
	\text{Var}[W] = \text{Var}[W] E\Bigl[\sum_j e^{-2 r \tau_j}\Bigr] + \text{Var}\Bigl[\sum_j e^{-r \tau_j}\Bigr]
\]
Solving for $\text{Var}[W]$, 
\[
	\text{Var}[W] = \frac{\text{Var}[\sum_j e^{-r \tau_j}]}{1 - E[\sum_j e^{-2 r \tau_j}]}
\]
To simplify the denominator, note that 
\[ 
E\Bigl[\sum_{j=1}^{\chi_i} e^{-2 r \tau_j}\Bigr] = E[\chi_i] E[e^{-2 r \tau_j}]
 = R_0 E[e^{-2 r \tau_j}]
 = R_0 \Bigl( \frac{\beta}{\beta + 2r}\Bigr )^\alpha 
 = R_0 \rho_2^\alpha 
 \]
Thus, the variance expression simplifies to 
\[
	\text{Var}[W] = \frac{\text{Var}[\sum_j e^{-r \tau_j}]}{1 - R_0 \rho_2^\alpha}
\]
The denominator does not depend on $\psi$, so we proceed by examining the numerator: 
\[
	\text{Var}[\sum_j e^{-r \tau_j}] = E[(\sum_j e^{-r \tau_j})^2] - E[\sum_j e^{-r \tau_j}]^2 = E[(\sum_j e^{- r \tau_j})^2] - 1
\]
(should the last term be $1/R_0$?)
Then, 
\[
	E[(\sum_j e^{- r \tau_j})^2] = E[\sum_j e^{-2 r \tau_j}] + E[\sum_{j \neq k} e^{-r (\tau_j + \tau_k)}]
\]
\[= R_0 \rho_2^\alpha + E[\chi_i (\chi_i - 1)] E[e^{-r \tau_j} e^{-r \tau_k}]\]
\[= R_0 \rho_2^\alpha + R_0^2 E[e^{-r \tau_j} e^{-r \tau_k}]\]
Since $\tau_j = l_i + \epsilon_j$, and since $l_i$, $\epsilon_j$, and $\epsilon_k$ are independent, 
\[ E[e^{-r \tau_j} e^{-r \tau_k}]  = E[e^{-2r l_i} e^{-r \epsilon_j} e^{-r \epsilon_k}] = E[e^{-2r l_i}] (E[e^{-r \epsilon}])^2 = \rho_2^{(1-\psi)\alpha} \cdot \rho ^{2 \psi \alpha}\]
Finally, we can assemble $\text{Var}[W]$: 
\[
	\text{Var}[W] = R_0 \rho_2^\alpha + R_0^2 \rho_2^{(1-\psi)\alpha} \rho^{2 \psi \alpha} - 1
\]
Substituting in the $z$ expressions for the $\rho$ parameters gives 
\begin{equation}
	\text{Var}[W] = \frac{
		\Bigl(\frac{1 + z}{1 + 2z}\Bigr)^\alpha + \Bigl(\frac{(1 + z)^2}{1 + 2z}\Bigr)^{(1-\psi) \alpha} - 1
	}{
		1 - \Bigl(\frac{1 + z}{1 + 2z}\Bigr)^\alpha
	}
\end{equation}
which is Eq.~\ref{eq:var_W}. 

\subsubsection*{Mode of the two-moment Gamma approximation for the time-shift distribution}

Following a simplified version of the moment-matching approach of 
\citet{morris_computation_2024}, we approximate the distribution of 
$W \mid \text{survival}$ by a Gamma distribution matched to its first 
two conditional moments. The unconditional moments of $W$ are:
%
\begin{align}
    E[W] &= 1 \\[6pt]
    E[W^2] &= \frac{\sigma^{(1-\psi)\alpha}}{1 - c^\alpha}
\end{align}
%
where the first follows from the Euler--Lotka equation ($E[S] = 1$ and 
the fixed-point equation), and the second follows from the variance 
calculation (since $E[W^2] = \text{Var}(W) + (E[W])^2 = \text{Var}(W) + 1$, 
combined with the expression for $\text{Var}(W)$ derived above). Conditioning 
on survival (i.e.\ on $W > 0$, which occurs with probability $p = 1 - q$ 
where $q$ is the extinction probability), the conditional moments are:
%
\begin{align}
    E[W \mid \text{surv}] &= \frac{E[W]}{p} = \frac{1}{p} 
    \label{eq:W_cond_mean} \\[6pt]
    E[W^2 \mid \text{surv}] &= \frac{E[W^2]}{p} 
    = \frac{\sigma^{(1-\psi)\alpha}}{p(1 - c^\alpha)}
    \label{eq:W_cond_second_moment}
\end{align}
%
from which the conditional variance and squared coefficient of variation 
(CV$^2$) follow:
%
\begin{align}
    \text{Var}(W \mid \text{surv}) 
    &= E[W^2 \mid \text{surv}] - \bigl(E[W \mid \text{surv}]\bigr)^2 
     = \frac{\sigma^{(1-\psi)\alpha} - p}{p^2(1-c^\alpha)} \\[10pt]
    V \equiv \text{CV}^2(W \mid \text{surv}) 
    &= \frac{\text{Var}(W \mid \text{surv})}{\bigl(E[W \mid \text{surv}]\bigr)^2} 
     = \frac{p\,\sigma^{(1-\psi)\alpha}}{1 - c^\alpha} - 1
    \label{eq:V_def}
\end{align}
%
We approximate $W \mid \text{surv}$ by a $\text{Gamma}(k, \lambda)$ 
distribution, matching its mean and CV$^2$ to those of $W \mid \text{surv}$. 
The Gamma distribution has mean $k/\lambda$ and CV$^2$ equal to $1/k$, so 
matching to Eqs.~\eqref{eq:W_cond_mean} and \eqref{eq:V_def} gives:
%
\begin{align}
    k &= \frac{1}{V} = \frac{1 - c^\alpha}{p\,\sigma^{(1-\psi)\alpha} - (1-c^\alpha)}
    \label{eq:k} \\[6pt]
    \lambda &= \frac{k}{E[W \mid \text{surv}]} = \frac{p}{V}
    \label{eq:lambda}
\end{align}
%
Both parameters are fully determined by $R_0$ (through $p$, $c$, and 
$\sigma$), $\alpha$, and $\psi$. The shape parameter $k = 1/V$ has a 
natural interpretation: it is the reciprocal of the conditional CV$^2$ 
of $W$, so that more variable epidemics (larger $V$, more punctuated 
profiles) correspond to smaller $k$ and hence more right-skewed 
approximating distributions. The approximation is valid as a proper 
unimodal density when $k \geq 1$ (i.e.\ $V \leq 1$); when $k < 1$ the 
approximating density is monotonically decreasing on $(0, \infty)$ with 
no interior mode, indicating that the most probable surviving epidemic 
is maximally delayed relative to the deterministic trajectory.

The time-shift $\varsigma = \log(W)/r$ is a monotone increasing 
transformation of $W$, so its modal value is obtained by transforming 
the mode of the Gamma approximation. The mode of $\text{Gamma}(k, \lambda)$ 
is $(k-1)/\lambda$ for $k \geq 1$, giving:
%
\begin{equation}
    \text{mode}(W \mid \text{surv}) 
    = \frac{k - 1}{\lambda} = \frac{1/V - 1}{p/V} = \frac{1 - V}{p}
\end{equation}
%
and therefore:
%
\begin{equation}
    \text{Mode}[\varsigma] 
    = \frac{1}{r}\log\!\left(\frac{1-V}{p}\right)
    = \frac{\log(1-V) - \log p}{r}
    \label{eq:mode_varsigma_split}
\end{equation}
%
Substituting $V$ from Eq.~\eqref{eq:V_def} and expressing in terms of 
$z = R_0^{1/\alpha} - 1$, this yields Eq.~\eqref{eq:mode_timeshift}. 
The split form of Eq.~\eqref{eq:mode_varsigma_split} is interpretable: 
the $\log(1-V)$ term carries all dependence on $\psi$, $\alpha$, and 
$R_0$ through the conditional CV$^2$ $V$, while the $-\log p$ term is 
a positive, $\psi$-independent correction arising from conditioning on 
survival. For $R_0 \geq 2$, the extinction probability $q$ is small 
($q \leq 0.20$) and $-\log p \leq 0.22$, so the survival correction 
is modest and the $\psi$-dependence of $\text{Mode}[\varsigma]$ is 
dominated by $\log(1-V)/r$.

\subsubsection*{Monotonicity in $R_0$}

Differentiating Eq.~\eqref{eq:alpha_log_sigma} with respect to $R_0$,  holding $\alpha$ fixed, and using 
$\frac{d}{dR_0}(2R_0^{1/\alpha}) = (2/\alpha)R_0^{1/\alpha - 1}$:
%
\begin{equation}
    \frac{\partial(\alpha\log\sigma)}{\partial R_0} 
    = \frac{2}{R_0} - \frac{2R_0^{1/\alpha - 1}}{2R_0^{1/\alpha} - 1}
    = \frac{2}{R_0} \cdot \frac{R_0^{1/\alpha} - 1}{2R_0^{1/\alpha} - 1}
    = \frac{2(\mu - 1)}{R_0(2\mu - 1)}
\end{equation}
%
This is strictly positive for all $R_0 > 1$, since $\mu = R_0^{1/\alpha} > 1$  makes both the numerator $\mu - 1$ and denominator $2\mu - 1$ positive. The  derivative vanishes at $R_0 = 1$ ($\mu = 1$), confirming that at the epidemic  threshold there is no $\psi$-dependence in either $\text{Var}(W)$ or  $\text{Mode}[\varsigma]$.

\subsubsection*{Monotonicity in $\alpha$}

Differentiating Eq.~\eqref{eq:alpha_log_sigma} with respect to $\alpha$,  holding $R_0$ fixed. Write $g(\alpha) = \alpha\log(2R_0^{1/\alpha} - 1)$,  so that $\alpha\log\sigma = 2\log R_0 - g(\alpha)$ and it suffices to show  $\partial g/\partial\alpha > 0$. By the product rule, using  $\frac{\partial}{\partial\alpha}R_0^{1/\alpha}  = -R_0^{1/\alpha}\log R_0/\alpha^2$:
%
\begin{equation}
    \frac{\partial g}{\partial\alpha} 
    = \log(2\mu - 1) - \frac{2\mu\log\mu}{2\mu - 1}
\end{equation}
%
where we have substituted $\mu = R_0^{1/\alpha}$ and $\log R_0 = \alpha\log\mu$.  To establish the sign, define  $h(\mu) = (2\mu-1)\log(2\mu-1) - 2\mu\log\mu$, so that the condition  $\partial g/\partial\alpha > 0$ is equivalent to $h(\mu) > 0$.  Differentiating:
%
\begin{equation}
    h'(\mu) = 2\log(2\mu - 1) + 2 - 2\log\mu - 2 
            = 2\log\!\left(2 - \frac{1}{\mu}\right)
\end{equation}
%
For $\mu > 1$, we have $2 - 1/\mu > 1$, so $h'(\mu) > 0$. Since $h(1) = 0$  and $h'(\mu) > 0$ for all $\mu > 1$, it follows that $h(\mu) > 0$ for all  $\mu > 1$, giving $\partial g/\partial\alpha > 0$ and hence:
%
\begin{equation}
    \frac{\partial(\alpha\log\sigma)}{\partial\alpha} < 0
\end{equation}
%
So $\alpha\log\sigma$ is strictly decreasing in $\alpha$: more concentrated  generation intervals (larger $\alpha$, holding $R_0$ fixed) reduce the  impact of punctuation on both $\text{Var}(W)$ and $\text{Mode}[\varsigma]$.

\subsection*{The modulating role of $\alpha \log \sigma$}

The $\psi$-dependent term in both $\text{Var}(W)$ and $\text{Mode}[\varsigma]$  is $\sigma^{(1-\psi)\alpha}$, which can be written as  $e^{(1-\psi)\alpha\log\sigma}$. The quantity $\alpha\log\sigma$ therefore  acts as the fundamental modulator of $\psi$-dependence: larger  $\alpha\log\sigma$ means the base $\sigma > 1$ is raised to a larger  effective power, amplifying the spread in $\text{Var}(W)$ and  $\text{Mode}[\varsigma]$ between the spike ($\psi \to 0$) and smooth  ($\psi \to 1$) limits. We can express this quantity exactly in terms of  $R_0$ and $\alpha$ alone. Starting from $\sigma = \mu^2/(2\mu-1)$ with  $\mu = R_0^{1/\alpha}$:
%
\begin{equation}
    \log\sigma = 2\log\mu - \log(2\mu - 1) 
               = \frac{2\log R_0}{\alpha} - \log(2R_0^{1/\alpha} - 1)
\end{equation}
%
and multiplying through by $\alpha$:
%
\begin{equation}
    \alpha\log\sigma = 2\log R_0 - \alpha\log(2R_0^{1/\alpha} - 1).
    \label{eq:alpha_log_sigma}
\end{equation}
%
We now show that this expression is strictly increasing in $R_0$ and  strictly decreasing in $\alpha$, confirming the qualitative picture that  punctuation ($\psi < 1$) matters more when $R_0$ is large and the  generation interval is dispersed (small $\alpha$).

\subsubsection*{Summary}

The two partial derivatives establish that the modulating quantity  $\alpha\log\sigma$ --- and hence the sensitivity of $\text{Var}(W)$ and  $\text{Mode}[\varsigma]$ to $\psi$ --- is:
%
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Increasing in $R_0$}: higher transmissibility amplifies  the effect of punctuation, because more offspring more densely sample  the individual infectiousness profile, inheriting more of its temporal  structure.
    \item \textbf{Decreasing in $\alpha$}: more concentrated generation  intervals dampen the effect of punctuation, because there is less  temporal variation across the generation interval for the shared  latent period $l_i$ to exploit.
\end{itemize}
%
Together, these results confirm that punctuated infectiousness  ($\psi < 1$) has the greatest impact on epidemic timing variability  when $R_0$ is large and the generation interval is dispersed ---  precisely the regime in which the individual infectiousness profile  has the most temporal structure for offspring timing to inherit.
