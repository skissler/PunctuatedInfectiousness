But: no household structure, no crowding, no production losses, COVID-specific. 

 2. Henry et al. (2025, Scientific Reports) — The paper Naia flagged (PMC12022078). An agent-based model of COVID-19 in food production facilities evaluating intervention strategies. But: single-facility scale (~100 workers), no household structure, retrospective/COVID-specific, no crop-level production impacts. 

 3. Lusk & Chandra (2021, PLOS ONE) — Already cited in your manuscript (the $309M figure). They estimated ag worker COVID burden by proportional allocation from county case rates, then translated  to output losses via labor cost-shares. But: no mechanistic disease model, no differential epidemiology, retrospective. 

 4. Charlton (2022, Applied Economic Perspectives and Policy) — Econometric evidence that agricultural labor concentrations amplified county-level COVID cases. Empirical support for the premise, but not a disease model.

Our model holds transmission rates constant over the course of an outbreak, which does not capture behavioral responses such as social distancing or school closures that typically reduce transmission as an epidemic progresses. However, agricultural workers may have less capacity to modify their behavior during an outbreak due to the in-person nature of their work, limited access to paid sick leave, and employer pressure to maintain operations. The constant-transmission assumption may therefore be more realistic for the agricultural workforce — the population of primary interest — than for the general community. Moreover, while we fix the basic reproduction number at the national level, effective transmission dynamics vary across regions and subpopulations through differences in household size and crowding distributions, capturing an important dimension of heterogeneity even under constant transmission rates.
