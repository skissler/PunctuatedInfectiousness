\section{The impact of punctuated infectiousness profiles on uncontrolled epidemics}

\subsection{Punctuated individual infectiousness profiles generate later and more variably-timed epidemics.}

Early-outbreak stochasticity can impact when an epidemic becomes established, which in turn shifts the epidemic's full trajectory\citep{morris_computation_2024}. Simulations demonstrate that epidemics with narrow individual infectiousness profiles ($\psi \rightarrow 0$) are more variably timed and tend to fall later than those with smooth profiles ($\psi \rightarrow 1$) ({\bf Figure \ref{fig:cuminf}}). To illustrate this, we measured the time required to reach 1,000 total cases (a measure of epidemic establishment, marking 10\% of the population infected) across 1,000 simulations for each of our three benchmark pathogens (influenza, SARS-CoV-2 omicron, and measles). We identified the 5\%, 50\% (median), and 95\% quantiles of these times-to-1,000-cases for $\psi = 0$, $\psi = 0.5$, and $\psi = 1$. For all three pathogens, the median time-to-1,000-cases shifted later, and the width of the (5\%, 95\%) quantile interval increased, as $\psi \rightarrow 0$ ({\bf Figure \ref{fig:cuminf}, Supplementary Table XX)}. This translated into meaningful differences in epidemic timing, particularly for SARS-CoV-2 omicron and measles: by day 21, XX\% of simulated SARS-CoV-2 omicron outbreaks with $\psi = 1$ had reached 1000 cases, while only XX\% of outbreaks with $\psi = 0$ had reached the same threshold; similarly, by day 28, XX\% of simulated measles outbreaks with $\psi = 1$ had reached 1000 cases, while only XX\% of outbreaks with $\psi = 0$ had reached the same threshold ({\bf Figure \ref{fig:survival}}).

Intuition suggests that the time shift's variance and its ``typical'' value should depend on $R_0$ and the skewness of $A(\tau)$. [Order statistics making smooth profiles have earlier hits]. [Low R0 causes the distinction between narrow and smooth profiles to collapse] [High R0 makes it so that, if a narrow profile happens to fall in the tail of the generation interval distribution, it injects lots of cases right at that point, causing a late shift]. The simulations align with this intuition: the (5\%, 95\%) quantile interval is most sensitive to $\psi$ for measles (largest $R_0$) and least sensitive to $\psi$ for influenza (smallest $R_0$). The median time shift is most sensitive to $\psi$ for SARS-CoV-2 omicron (largest skewness, with intermediate $R_0$). 

Analytic results confirm that the time shift's variance increases, and its modal value shifts later, with narrower infectiousness profiles. Furthermore, the size of these effects depends on $R_0$ and the skewness of $A(\tau)$. To demonstrate this, we use branching process theory \citep{morris_computation_2024}. During an epidemic's exponential growth phase, the cumulative incidence over time, $Z(t)$, can be approximated as a deterministic exponential function with random initial scaling factor $W$:
% Let  contacts be uniform over time and across individuals ($c_i(t) \equiv 1 \, \forall \, i$), and let $\xi_i(\tau) $ follow the time-shifted Gamma model (Eq.~\ref{eq:gamma_timeshift}). Drawing upon branching process theory, the cumulative incidence over time, $Z(t)$, during the exponential growth phase can be approximated as a deterministic exponential function with a random initial scaling factor $W$: 
%
\begin{equation}
	Z(t) \approx W \cdot e^{rt}
	\label{eq:zt}
\end{equation}
%
where $r$ is the early-outbreak exponential growth rate. Through a change of variables, the random scaling factor $W$ can be expressed as a random time shift: 
%  $E[W]$ is the initial number of infectious individuals, $I_0$. For clarity of the derivation, we use $E[W]=I_0=1$ throughout. 
%
\begin{equation}
	Z(t) \approx e^{r(t + \varsigma)}
	\label{eq:zt_timeshift}
\end{equation}
%
where
%
% \begin{equation}
% 	\varsigma = \frac{\log W - \log E[W]}{r}
% 	\label{eq:timeshift}
% \end{equation}
% \begin{equation}
% 	\varsigma = \frac{\log W - \log E[W]}{r} = \frac{\log W}{r}
% 	\label{eq:timeshift}
% \end{equation}
% %
% when $E[W] = 1$
\begin{equation}
	\varsigma = \frac{\log W - \log E[W]}{r}
	\label{eq:timeshift}
\end{equation}
%
We note that $E[W]$ is the initial number of cases, $I_0$, which for simplicity we assume to be 1 throughout this derivation; thus, $\varsigma \propto \log W $. Our goal is to describe the properties of $\varsigma$. 

First, we seek an expression for $\text{Var}[W]$ as a function of $\psi$, noting large $\text{Var}[W]$ implies large $\text{Var}[\varsigma]$ and {\it vice versa}. Using the distributional fixed point equation, which relies on the fact that the branching processes originating from each of the index case's offspring are statistical copies of the branching process originating from the index case, we can compute $\text{Var}[W]$ directly ({\bf Supplementary Methods}): 
%
\begin{equation}
	\text{Var}[W] = \frac{
		\Bigl(\frac{1 + z}{1 + 2z}\Bigr)^\alpha + \Bigl(\frac{(1 + z)^2}{1 + 2z}\Bigr)^{(1-\psi) \alpha} - 1
	}{
		1 - \Bigl(\frac{1 + z}{1 + 2z}\Bigr)^\alpha
	} := \frac{\rho_1^\alpha + \rho_2^{(1-\psi)\alpha} - 1}{1 - \rho_1^\alpha} \qquad \text{where } \rho_k = \frac{(1 + z)^k}{1 + 2z}
	\label{eq:var_W}
\end{equation}
%
where $z = R_0^{1/\alpha} - 1$. Thus, $\text{Var}[W]$ depends on $R_0$, $\alpha$ (\ie, the shape/skewness of $A(\tau)$), and $\psi$. The punctuation parameter $\psi$ enters Eq.~\ref{eq:var_W} only once --- in the exponent of $\rho_2$ --- and $\rho_2$ is strictly positive for $R_0 > 1$; thus, $\text{Var}[W]$ and $\psi$ are monotonically related, where narrower individual infectiousness profiles ($\psi \rightarrow 0$) maximize the variance and broader profiles ($\psi \rightarrow 1$) minimize it. Furthermore, $\rho_2$ is monotonically increasing in $R_0$ ({\bf Supplementary Methods}), so larger $R_0$ amplifies the dependence of $\text{Var}[W]$ on $\psi$. 

% where $z = r \bar{g} / \alpha$, and $\bar{g}$ is the mean of the generation interval distribution $g(\tau)$. Note that, from the Euler-Lotka equation, $z = R_0^{1/\alpha} - 1$, so increasing $z$ implies increasing $R_0$ (for fixed $\alpha$). The punctuation parameter $\psi$ enters the expression only once, in the middle term of the numerator, demonstrating that $\text{Var}[W]$ and $\psi$ are monotonically related: narrower profiles ($\psi \rightarrow 0$) maximize the variance, while broader profiles ($\psi \rightarrow 1$) minimize it. The parameter $z$ can be interpreted as the amount of epidemic growth per generation ($r \bar{g}$) multiplied by the generation interval distribution's coefficient of variation ($1/\alpha$). Thus, $z$ increases with faster-growing epidemics, longer mean generation times, and more variable generation interval distributions (under fixed $\bar{g}$). 

We can also derive an approximate expression for the modal value of $\varsigma$ using a two-moment version of the moment matching approach of \citet{morris_computation_2024} ({\bf Supplementary Methods}): 
% These moments are 
% %
% \begin{equation}
% 	E[W] = 1, \qquad E[W^2] = \frac{\Bigl(\frac{(1 + z)^2}{1 + 2z}\Bigr)^{(1-\psi) \alpha}}{1 - \Bigl(\frac{1 + z}{1 + 2z}\Bigr)^\alpha}
% \end{equation}
% %
% where the first follows from the property $E[W] = I_0$ and the second follows from the variance calculation derived above, with $E[W^2] = \text{Var}[W] + (E[W])^2 = \text{Var}[W] + 1$. With these moments in hand it can be shown ({\bf Supplementary Methods}) that
%
\begin{equation}
	\text{Mode}[\varsigma] = \frac{1}{r} \log \Biggl( 
	\frac{
		2 \bigl[1 - \bigl(\frac{1 + z}{1 + 2z}\bigr)^\alpha\bigr] - p \Bigl(\frac{(1 + z)^2}{1 + 2z}\Bigr)^{(1-\psi)\alpha}
	}{
		p\bigl[1 - \bigl(\frac{1 + z}{1 + 2z}\bigr)^\alpha\bigr]
	}
	\Biggr) = \frac{1}{r} \log \Biggl(
	\frac{2 (1 - \rho_1^\alpha) - p \rho_2^{(1-\psi) \alpha}}{p (1 - \rho_1^\alpha)}
	\Biggr)
	\label{eq:mode_timeshift}
\end{equation}
%
provided the numerator within the logarithm is positive; when the numerator is zero or negative, no finite modal time shift exists and  $\text{Mode}[\varsigma]  \rightarrow -\infty$. Again, $\psi$ enters the expression only once, with narrow individual infectiousness profiles ($\psi \rightarrow 0$) minimizing the mode (making it more negative, \ie, causing the typical epidemic to be shifted later) and broad individual infectiousness profiles ($\psi \rightarrow 1$) maximizing it (causing the typical epidemic to be shifted earlier). As before, larger $R_0$ increases $\rho_2$ and thus amplifies the dependence of $\text{Mode}[\varsigma]$ on $\psi$.

% \end{equation}

\subsection{Narrow biological infectiousness profiles interact with periodic contact patterns to generate overdispersion in secondary infections}

When contacts are uniform in time, the ``punctuatedness'' of biological infectiousness ($\psi$) has no effect on the secondary infection distribution: every individual produces a $\text{Poisson}(R_0)$-distributed number of infections, and there is no overdispersion ($k \rightarrow \infty$) irrespective of $\psi$. However, if contacts vary over time, narrower biological profiles ``see'' more of this contact variation, generating overdispersion in the secondary infection distribution. We illustrate this using the periodic contact model, $c_i(t) = 1 - A \cos(\omega t)$, for which a closed-form expression for $k$ can be obtained ({\bf Supplementary Methods}):
%
\begin{equation}
	k = \frac{2}{A^2}\left(1 + \frac{\omega^2}{\beta^2}\right)^{\psi \alpha}
	\label{eq:k_periodic_main}
\end{equation}
%
where $A$ is the amplitude of the contact oscillation and $\omega$ is its angular frequency. Here, the prefactor $2/A^2$ sets the overall overdispersion scale, and the bracketed factor quantifies the failure of the biological profile's temporal window to average out the contact oscillation, with $(1 + \omega^2/\beta^2)^{-\psi\alpha}$ being exactly the squared characteristic function of the Gamma$(\psi\alpha, \beta)$ density at the contact frequency $\omega$. Since this factor is strictly increasing in $\psi$ for any $\omega > 0$, narrower biological profiles always produce more overdispersion in the presence of periodic contact variation. The effect disappears in the spike limit only if $\omega \rightarrow 0$ or $A \rightarrow 0$ (no temporal contact variation), and grows arbitrarily large in the smooth limit if $\omega/\beta$ is large (contact oscillations much faster than the profile window, so the profile averages them out). Simulations extend this result to heavier-tailed, non-periodic contact heterogeneity, for which the same mechanism holds qualitatively ({\bf Figure XX}).
