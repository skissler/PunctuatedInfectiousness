\section{The impact of punctuated infectiousness profiles on uncontrolled epidemics}

\subsection{Punctuated individual infectiousness profiles generate later and more variably-timed epidemics.}

Even in large popualations, early-outbreak stochasticity can impact when an epidemic becomes established and begins its exponential growth phase \citep{morris_computation_2024}. This time shift durably impacts the epidemic's full trajectory. The effect of this early-outbreak stochasticity can be modeled as a random time shift \citep{morris_computation_2024}. Here, we demonstrate that the distribution of this time shift --- both its mode and variance --- depend on $\psi$. 

Let the contact potential be constant in time and uniform across individuals ($c_i(t) = R_0$), and let $b_i(\tau) $ follow the time-shifted Gamma model (Eq.~\ref{eq:gamma_timeshift}). Drawing upon branching process theory, the cumulative incidence over time, $Z(t)$, during the exponential growth phase can be approximated as a deterministic exponential function with a random initial scaling factor $W$: 
%
\begin{equation}
	Z(t) \approx W \cdot e^{rt}
	\label{eq:zt}
\end{equation}
%
Here, $r$ is the exponential growth rate and $E[W]$ is the initial number of infectious individuals (for clarity of the derivation, we use $E[W]=1$ throughout). 

The random initial condition $W$ can, through a change of variables, be expressed as a random time shift: 
%
\begin{equation}
	Z(t) \approx e^{r(t + \varsigma)}
	\label{eq:zt_timeshift}
\end{equation}
%
where
%
\begin{equation}
	\varsigma = \frac{\log W - \log E[W]}{r}
	\label{eq:timeshift}
\end{equation}
%
Since $E[W]$ and $r$ are constants, $\varsigma$ and $W$ are monotonically related. 

First, we derive an expression for $\text{Var}[W]$, noting that $\text{Var}[W] \propto \text{Var}[\varsigma]$.

\end{equation}
%
Thus, narrower infectiousness profiles ($\psi \rightarrow 0$) yield more variable epidemic establishment times, and thus more variably-timed epidemics overall. This effect is magnified as $R_0$ increases. 

We can also derive an expression for the mode of the distribution of $\varsigma$: 
%

\end{equation}
%
Thus, narrower infectiousness profiles ($psi \rightarrow 0$) cause the typical (modal) epidemic to be shifted later in time. This efffect is also magnified as $R_0$ increases. 

Stochastic simulations confirm the analytic results. The time to 100 infections varies with $\psi$, especially for pathogens with high $R_0$ (SARS-CoV-2 omicron, measles). Thus, punctuation of the individual infectiousness profile can substantially impact the timing of epidemic establishment, especially when $R_0$ is high.
